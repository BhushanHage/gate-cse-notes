# Physical Layer

The Physical Layer is the first layer in the OSI model and is responsible for the transmission and reception of unstructured raw data between a sender and a receiver. This layer deals with the physical connection between devices and transmits data over various transmission media.

## Key Concepts:

### 1. Signals
- **Analog Signals**: Continuous signals that vary over time, representing physical quantities such as sound, temperature, etc.
- **Digital Signals**: Discrete signals that represent data as binary values (0s and 1s).

### 2. Transmission Media
- **Guided Media**: Physical medium through which signals are transmitted; includes twisted pair cables, coaxial cables, and optical fiber.
- **Unguided Media**: Wireless transmission methods such as radio waves, microwaves, and infrared.

### 3. Bandwidth
- The range of frequencies over which a signal is transmitted. Bandwidth determines the amount of data that can be transmitted in a given time period.

### 4. Baud Rate vs Bit Rate
- **Baud Rate**: The number of signal changes (symbols) per second. 
- **Bit Rate**: The number of bits transmitted per second. 
  
  Bit Rate can be higher than Baud Rate when each signal change represents multiple bits.

### 5. Multiplexing
- The technique used to send multiple signals over a single communication channel. Includes:
  - **Time Division Multiplexing (TDM)**: Divides time among multiple signals.
  - **Frequency Division Multiplexing (FDM)**: Divides frequency bands among multiple signals.

### 6. Line Coding
- The process of converting digital data into a digital signal. Types include:
  - **NRZ (Non-Return to Zero)**
  - **Manchester Encoding**

### 7. Guided and Unguided Media
- **Guided Media**: Include twisted pair cables (used in telephones), coaxial cables (used in cable TV), and fiber optics (high-speed internet).
- **Unguided Media**: Use air as the medium; examples include radio waves (broadcasting) and microwave frequencies (cell phones).

### 8. Propagation
- The way signals travel through the medium. Signal can propagate in various modes such as ground, space, and line-of-sight.

### 9. Attenuation
- The loss of signal strength as it travels through the medium. Attenuation increases with distance and can be influenced by frequency.

### 10. Noise
- Any unwanted signal that interferes with the communication channel. Types include:
  - **Thermal Noise**: Caused by the agitation of electrons.
  - **Interference**: From other communication circuits or devices.
